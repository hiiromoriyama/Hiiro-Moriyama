Megahertz Tomoscopy

This work was conducted as part of the 2023 Summer Research Program at DESY, where I joined the SPB/SFX group at the European XFEL to contribute to the Megahertz Tomoscopy project, supervised by Dr. Patrik Vagovic and Dr. Tokushi Sato, and directed by Professor Henry Chapman.

X-ray Optics for Imaging Fast Procecess in 3D

The discovery of X-rays by Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen ushered in a new era of medical and scientific imaging. With advancements, researchers now use high-energy accelerators, including X-ray Free Electron Lasers (XFEL) and 4th generation synchrotrons. An XFEL produces coherent ultra-short X-rays pulses, often less than 100 femtoseconds. Maintaining or modifying the inherent characteristics of the X-ray beam for experimental suitability presents considerable challenges. At European XFEL in Hamburg, Germany, researchers have started their preliminary analysis of the potential of the Megahertz X-ray Multi-Projection Imaging prototype (MHz-XMPI). This innovative technique employs a single XFEL pulse, channeled through diamond beam splitters to the sample, enabling the examination from multiple angles through a time-delayed approach. Once the XFEL pulse interacts with the sample, utilizing the Bragg Magnifier Microscope (BMM), made of highly asymmetric germanium crystals, for geometrical magnification becomes essential to enhance the spatial resolution of the image. Combining BMM with a direct detection detector offers a high spatial resolution with high sensitivity and dynamic range. This is significant, as current indirect detectors are limited to a 10-bit dynamic range with a resolution of 5 micrometer. However, with these optics, we can achieve a 0.3 micrometer resolution and a 16-bit dynamic range, which is promising for XMPI imaging.
